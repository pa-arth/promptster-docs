## From telemetry to judgment

Promptster derives structured, hiring-relevant evidence from a candidate's session and organizes it into the **AI Fluency rubric** (`ai_fluency_v1`). The rubric answers *how* the candidate worked with AI — how they framed the task, directed the model, steered it when it drifted, and verified the result — not just what they shipped.

<Note>
  No part of the rubric penalizes AI usage. A candidate who leans on AI heavily but prompts well, steers deliberately, and verifies their work rates highly. Promptster measures *how well* a candidate works with AI, not whether they use it.
</Note>

<Info>
  Rubric evaluation runs as a background job after the session ends. Allow a few minutes after `promptster done` for signals and ratings to appear.
</Info>

## The fluency dimensions

The rubric scores eight dimensions, grouped by the phase of work they describe. Each is evidence-backed and rated independently.

### Discovery

| Dimension | The question it answers |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| `task_framing` | Did the candidate establish a clear, well-scoped goal before acting? |
| `comprehension_grounding` | Did they ground themselves in the codebase and confirm their understanding? |

### Implementation

| Dimension | The question it answers |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| `direction_quality` | Are the candidate's instructions to the AI clear, constrained, and well-decomposed? |
| `steering_discernment` | Do they catch drift, correct the model, and hold a sensible merge bar on what they accept? |

### Verification

| Dimension | The question it answers |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| `verification_loop` | Do they check the work — tests, builds, targeted validation — as they go, not just at the end? |

### Cross-cutting

| Dimension | The question it answers |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| `fix_integrity` | Does the change address the real problem rather than mask a symptom? |
| `context_management` | Do they give the model enough context without overloading it? |
| `ecosystem_leverage` | Do they use the tools around them — subagents, MCP tools, the wider toolchain — effectively? |

<Note>
  `fix_integrity` requires inspecting diff content, so it is evaluated only where that content is available. When a dimension can't be collected for a session, it is reported as `not_collectible` — never as a low rating.
</Note>

## How each dimension is rated

Every dimension carries a **tier** (the rating) and a **confidence** (how much evidence backs it).

| Tier | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| `strong` | Clear, consistent evidence of the behavior. |
| `adequate` | The behavior is present and sufficient. |
| `developing` | Some evidence, but inconsistent or thin. |
| `insufficient_evidence` | The behavior is observable in principle, but this session didn't produce enough to judge. |
| `not_collectible` | The capture tool structurally can't emit the evidence for this dimension — never counts against the candidate. |

| Confidence | Meaning |
|------------|---------|
| `high` | Backed by ample, unambiguous evidence. |
| `medium` | Enough evidence to rate, with some caveats. |
| `low` | Rated from limited evidence — weigh accordingly. |

Treat `insufficient_evidence` and `not_collectible` as *absence of data*, not as poor performance. They are common in short sessions or when a tool doesn't emit a given event type.

## The evidence behind the ratings

Two artifacts feed the rubric. You can read them directly to see *why* a dimension landed where it did.

### `data_points_v1` — deterministic metrics

Flat, countable metrics computed straight from the timeline — no model judgment involved. Among them:

| Metric | What it measures |
|--------|------------------|
| `promptCount` | Prompts sent to the AI. |
| `commandCount` / `commandFailRate` | Commands run, and the fraction that failed. |
| `fileDiffCount` / `uniqueFilesTouched` | Volume and spread of changes. |
| `firstChangeLatencyMs` | Time from session start to the first file edit — a proxy for orienting before acting. |
| `verifyIntensity` | Verification commands (tests, lint, build) relative to changes made. |
| `manualEditRatio` | Fraction of changes made by hand versus accepted from the AI. |
| `errorEpisodeCount` / `recoveryCount` | How often work broke, and how often the candidate recovered. |
| `explorationBeforeActionRatio` / `readBeforeWriteRatio` | How much the candidate read and explored before writing. |

`data_points_v1` also carries `cost`, `testResults`, and an overall `confidence` (`high` / `medium` / `low`).

### `fluency_signals_v1` — attributed, phase-nested signals

Richer signals nested by **phase** (discovery, implementation, verification) and, critically, by **actor**:

- **`candidate.*`** — human-attributable behavior (for example `candidate.discovery.comprehensionPromptCount`, `candidate.implementation.correctivePromptCount`, `candidate.verification.redGreenArcCount`). These can raise or lower a tier.
- **`agentContext.*`** — model-autonomous activity (for example `agentContext.aiDiffCount`, `agentContext.subagentDispatchCount`). These provide context for the reviewer but **never score the human** — work the model did on its own is not credited to or held against the candidate.

This separation is what lets the rubric evaluate *the candidate's* judgment rather than the model's throughput.

## Using signals well

- **Read the evidence, not just the tier.** A `developing` `verification_loop` with `verifyIntensity` near zero tells a different story than one with high `verifyIntensity` but late timing.
- **Respect confidence.** A `strong` rating at `low` confidence is a hint, not a verdict — corroborate it in the [timeline](/reviewers/sessions/overview).
- **Compare within a cohort.** Ratings and metrics are most meaningful relative to other candidates on the same assessment. See [Cohort analysis](/reviewers/guides/cohort-analysis).
